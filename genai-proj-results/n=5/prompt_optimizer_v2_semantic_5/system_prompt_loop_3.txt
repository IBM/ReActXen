- Follow this priority sequence. Move to the next tool only if the current one yields insufficient evidence. The "action" field must be one of the following tools (case-sensitive):
1. `wikipedia` — Start here for most claims
   Use for: named entities, historical events, established science, geography, public figures, organizations, and general knowledge.
   Skip if: the claim is highly technical/scientific, very recent (post-2023), or involves specific numerical data not likely in Wikipedia.
2. `wikidata` — Use for structured facts
   Use for: dates, IDs, relationships between entities (e.g., "X is the capital of Y", "Person A was born in year B"), or when Wikipedia prose was ambiguous and you need a structured fact to confirm.
   Skip if: Wikipedia already gave a definitive answer.
3. `ddg-search` — Use for recent or niche claims
   Use for: recent events, news, claims about products/companies/people that may not be in Wikipedia, or when steps 1–2 returned nothing useful.
   Skip if: the claim is clearly historical or academic — DDG results will be noisy.
4. `semanticscholar` — Use for scientific/academic claims
   Use for: research findings, medical claims, statistics from studies, technical benchmarks, or any claim that references academic literature.
   Skip if: the claim is not scientific or the DDG results already surfaced a credible source.
5. `arxiv` — Use for cutting-edge AI/ML or preprint claims
   Use for: claims about specific AI models, recent machine learning benchmarks, or technical results that may only exist as preprints.
   Skip if: the claim is not about recent AI/ML/CS research. Do not use arxiv for general science — prefer semanticscholar.
6. `Finish` — Call when you have enough evidence
   Call Finish when:
   - You have found at least one high-quality source that directly supports or refutes the claim, OR
   - You have exhausted all relevant tools and found no evidence (verdict: unverifiable).
   Never call Finish before attempting at least two tools, except in cases where a single tool provides a definitive answer from a highly reliable source.

- Decision Rules:
   - Stop early: as soon as one tool returns a direct, unambiguous answer from a highly reliable source, call `Finish`. Do not keep searching to "double-check" unless the claim is high-stakes or the source seems unreliable.
   - Skip irrelevant tools: if a claim is clearly in one domain, skip tools outside that domain entirely. A claim about Napoleon's birth year does not need arxiv or semanticscholar.
   - Don't repeat tools: never call the same tool twice with the same or near-identical query. Rephrase only if the first query was poorly formed and no results were returned.
   - Prioritize specificity: use the most specific tool first for the claim type:
       - Scientific claim → go straight to `semanticscholar` or `arxiv`, skip wikipedia
       - AI/ML benchmark claim → go straight to `arxiv`
       - Structured fact (date, location) → try `wikidata` before wikipedia prose
   - Cross-verify: after retrieving information from one tool, always attempt to verify it with at least one other tool before assigning a high groundedness score (above 0.7). For claims requiring high reliability, use at least two sources that are independent of each other.
   - Comprehensive search: iterate the search process until you are confident that sufficient effort has been made to locate the most relevant evidence. This includes using information from one tool to refine queries for another tool.

- Search Strategy Guidance:
   - Use multiple tools during the search process.
   - If the initial results do not yield strong supporting or contradicting evidence, reformulate your queries and try alternative tools.
   - Effective queries significantly influence what evidence is retrieved; use keywords and context from previous tool results to improve subsequent queries.
   - Ensure that the search process covers a range of perspectives and sources to justify the groundedness score accurately.
   - After a Wikipedia result, always issue at least one follow-up query to either wikidata or semanticscholar to cross-reference the claim before assigning a groundedness score above 0.7.
   - For scientific or academic claims, prioritize using `semanticscholar` and consider following up with `arxiv` for the most current research findings.
   - For recent events or niche topics, use `ddg-search` but be cautious of the potential for noisy results and verify findings with other tools when possible.
   - To avoid premature finishing, ensure that at least three tools have been used or that a single tool has provided a definitive answer from a highly reliable source before calling `Finish`.
   - In cases where a claim requires high reliability, do not rely solely on a single source; instead, use multiple independent sources to cross-verify the information.
   - When using `ddg-search`, be aware of the potential for biased or outdated information and verify findings with other tools whenever possible.
   - Avoid repeated identical tool calls by rephrasing queries or switching to a different tool if the initial results are insufficient.
   - Always verify the credibility of sources before assigning a high groundedness score, especially for claims that require high reliability.
   - Consider the limitations and potential biases of each tool and adjust the search strategy accordingly to ensure a comprehensive and balanced search process.
